    Knee pain at your desk: what’s really going on

    Reviewed by an OrthoNow orthopaedic specialist • 5 min read

    If your knee aches after a long day at a desk, you’re not imagining it. Sitting for hours with the knee bent at 90 degrees keeps the joint loaded in one position, reduces circulation to the cartilage, and shortens the muscles that stabilise the kneecap. The result is a dull ache that flares when you finally stand up.

    The good news: desk-related knee pain is usually mechanical, not structural. That means it responds well to movement, posture changes and targeted strengthening — long before anyone needs to talk about scans or surgery.

    Why the pain shows up after sitting

    When the knee stays bent, the patellofemoral joint — where the kneecap glides over the thigh bone — sits under constant, uneven pressure. Weak quadriceps and tight hamstrings pull the kneecap slightly out of its groove, and over hours that small misalignment becomes noticeable. Stand up, and the joint suddenly has to move through a range it hasn’t used in a while.

    People often assume the worst — a tear, arthritis, something that needs an operation. In reality, most desk-worker knee pain is a strength-and-mobility problem. That’s an important distinction, because the fix is in your control.

    What actually helps

    Three things move the needle for most people. First, break up sitting: stand and take ten steps every 30–40 minutes to reload the joint gently. Second, strengthen the quadriceps — straight-leg raises and wall sits build the support the kneecap needs. Third, loosen the hamstrings and hip flexors, which quietly pull on the knee when they’re tight.

    Set your chair so your hips are slightly above your knees and your feet rest flat. That single change reduces the bend angle and takes pressure off the joint through the day. If you can, alternate between sitting and a standing desk.

    When to get it checked

    Most desk-related knee pain settles within a few weeks of these changes. But some signals mean it’s worth seeing a specialist sooner rather than later: pain that wakes you at night, swelling that doesn’t go down, the knee locking or giving way, or pain that persists beyond three weeks despite the changes above.

    Those aren’t reasons to panic — they’re reasons to get a clear diagnosis. A specialist can tell in one visit whether you’re dealing with a simple overload or something that needs imaging, and either way you leave with a plan rather than a guess.

    The bottom line

    Knee pain from desk work is common, mechanical and usually very treatable without surgery. Move more often, strengthen the muscles around the joint, fix your chair height, and give it a couple of weeks. If it doesn’t settle — or if any of the warning signs show up — a single consultation will tell you exactly where you stand.
